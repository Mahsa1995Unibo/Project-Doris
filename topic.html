Topic & Context

Jewish Refugees in 1938:
The Historical Background

Understanding the events that shaped how newspapers described Jewish displacement — and why framing mattered then and now.

The Historical Context

By 1938, Jewish people in Germany and Austria were facing systematic persecution under the Nazi regime. The year was pivotal: three major events defined the international response — and generated intense newspaper coverage across all three languages in the mhKG.

Jul

Évian Conference · July 1938

32 countries met in France to discuss the Jewish refugee crisis. Almost all refused to raise immigration quotas. A defining moment of international failure.

Sep

Italian Racial Laws · Sep 1938

Italy passed antisemitic legislation modelled on Germany's Nuremberg Laws. This shifted Italian press rhetoric sharply.

Nov

Kristallnacht · Nov 9–10, 1938

Coordinated Nazi attacks destroyed thousands of Jewish businesses and synagogues. This is the diachronic anchor of our analysis.

Why Framing Matters

Two journalists can describe the same crowd of displaced people and produce completely different moral pictures — depending on which words they choose. This is framing: the way language shapes who we see as the victim and who as the threat.

A concrete example

"Anti-Nazi protesters charged the police lines."

→ Frame: ATTACK. Assailant = protesters. Victim = police.

The same event, different framing

"Crowds of anti-Nazis were dispersed by police forces."

→ Frame: DISPERSAL. Agent = police. Theme = crowds (passive).

The Three Corpora

Language Source Political position in 1938 Expected framing
EN English American newspapers (Library of Congress) Mixed — some sympathy, some restrictionism Jewish people likely as Victim
DE German Hakenkreuzbanner (Nazi party, Mannheim) Official Nazi ideology Jewish people as threat / Agent
IT Italian Italian press (DiaCORIS / Il Resto del Carlino) Fascist but less radical before Sept 1938 Shifting after racial laws

What Is FrameNet?

FrameNet is a linguistic database developed at UC Berkeley. It defines hundreds of frames — conceptual scenarios — each with defined participant roles called Frame Elements.

Frame example — ATTACK

The ATTACK frame is triggered by words like attack, assault, raid, charge. Its Frame Elements are: Assailant (who attacks), Victim (who is attacked), and optionally Weapon. The same word "attack" in a German article vs an English article may assign these roles to completely different entities.

The Knowledge Gap We Address

The mhKG encodes frame-role assignments for entities across three languages, but no study has yet systematically compared the Victim vs Assailant assignment specifically for Jewish entities across all three corpora in 1938 — before and after Kristallnacht. That is the gap this project fills.
