Methodology

How We Did It:
Step by Step

From exploring the Knowledge Graph to combining SPARQL results with LLMs — our complete analytical pipeline.

The Pipeline at a Glance

Overview
Explore KG → Define Research Questions → Write SPARQL Queries → Feed Results to LLMs → Evaluate → Document

Step 1 — Explore the mhKG

Before writing targeted queries, we ran exploration queries to understand what the Knowledge Graph actually contains. This is like reading a map before choosing a route.

a

What roles exist?

We listed all distinct semantic roles in the graph (Victim, Assailant, Agent, Theme…) and their frequency — this became our analytical vocabulary.

b

How many articles per language?

We counted articles per language to understand the corpus balance and normalise comparisons later.

c

Do our keywords appear?

We tested whether "jews", "juden", "ebrei", "refugees" actually appear in the graph before building full queries around them.

Step 2 — Define Research Questions

We defined four research questions, one per framing dimension required by the course:

DimensionOur Research Question
Cross-languageIs the Victim role assigned more often to Jewish entities in English than in German sources?
Cross-documentDo different newspapers covering Kristallnacht agree on who caused the violence?
DiachronicDoes framing of Jewish refugees change before vs after November 9, 1938?
AbsenceAre certain frames completely absent in one language but present in another?

Step 3 — SPARQL Queries

We wrote four SPARQL queries, collectively using all six mandatory keywords. Each query targets one of our research questions. See the full queries and results on the SPARQL page →

Keywords used
DISTINCT · OPTIONAL · UNION · FILTER · REGEX · LIMIT · ORDER BY

Step 4 — LLM Experiments

We took the SPARQL results (sets of RDF triples) and fed them to two LLMs using three prompting techniques. We compared the outputs and evaluated each approach.

TechniqueWhat we didLLMs used
Zero-shotAsked each LLM to interpret the triples with no examples or guidanceGPT-4o · Claude Sonnet
Few-shotProvided 2 example analyses before asking for the real oneGPT-4o · Claude Sonnet
Chain-of-thoughtAsked the LLM to reason step-by-step before answeringGPT-4o · Claude Sonnet

Step 5 — Evaluation

For every approach we tested, we scored the output across six dimensions: Accuracy, Analytical Depth, Consistency with KG, Bias Sensitivity, Usability, and Trustworthiness. See the full Evaluation page →

Tools Used

mhKG SPARQL Endpoint

The multilingual historical Knowledge Graph built by the course team. Queried directly with SPARQL.

GPT-4o

OpenAI's large language model. Used for all three prompting techniques.

Claude Sonnet

Anthropic's large language model. Compared directly against GPT-4o outputs.

FrameNet · Framester

Used to identify frame names and Frame Element URIs for our queries.
