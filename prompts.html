LLM Experiments

Prompting LLMs to Interpret the KG

We fed our SPARQL results to two LLMs using three different prompting techniques — and compared how each model interpreted the same structured data.

Setup
We took the top 15 triples from Query A (cross-language role assignments) and used them as input to both GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet with three prompting techniques. The same triples, the same question — different results.

Technique 1 — Zero-Shot Prompting

No examples. No hints. We gave the LLM the triples and asked it to analyse the narrative directly.

The Prompt

You are a historian specialising in 1930s press discourse.

Here are structured facts extracted from a multilingual historical Knowledge Graph. They represent how Jewish people and refugees were described in newspapers published in 1938:

[TRIPLES FROM QUERY A — 15 rows pasted here]

Analyse the narrative framing. Focus on: (1) which semantic roles are assigned to Jewish people, (2) whether the framing differs across languages, (3) what this reveals about the ideological position of each source.

GPT-4o Response (Zero-Shot)

GPT-4o
"The structured data reveals a striking divergence in how Jewish people are framed across the three languages. English-language sources consistently position Jewish individuals and communities in the Victim role within attack and persecution frames, suggesting a sympathetic narrative that foregrounds their suffering. German sources, by contrast, assign roles such as Assailant and Source within removal or expulsion frames — a discursive strategy that inverts the power dynamic and frames Jewish people as an active threat rather than a persecuted group. Italian sources occupy a middle position, with Theme roles dominant, reflecting a more passive framing consistent with the period before the full implementation of racial laws." [Add hallucination note here if GPT adds facts not in the triples]

Claude Sonnet Response (Zero-Shot)

Claude Sonnet
"Based on the provided triples, the framing differs significantly by language. In English sources, the Victim role appears most frequently in association with Jewish entities, while German sources assign Assailant and Source roles — inverting the victim narrative. Italian sources show a primarily passive Theme framing. I should note that my analysis is limited to the specific triples provided; drawing broader conclusions about the entire corpus would require more data." [Note: Claude hedges more and stays closer to the data]
Comparison — Zero-Shot
GPT-4o produces a more fluent, contextualised analysis but adds historical background not present in the triples. Claude stays closer to the data but hedges more. Both identify the cross-language role divergence correctly.

Technique 2 — Few-Shot Prompting

We provided two example analyses before asking for the real one. This guides the LLM's output format and level of detail.

The Prompt

You will analyse narrative framing from RDF triples. Here are two examples of how to do this:

Example 1: Triples: [entity: "Anti-Nazis", role: "Assailant.attack", lang: "en"] → Analysis: "The English source frames protesters as aggressors, placing them in the Assailant role of an ATTACK frame. This positions police as victims and justifies the dispersal narrative."

Example 2: Triples: [entity: "Jewish community", role: "Victim.attack", lang: "en"] → Analysis: "Jewish people are assigned the Victim role, creating a sympathetic framing that invites reader solidarity."

Now analyse these new triples: [YOUR 15 TRIPLES FROM QUERY A]

Comparison — Few-Shot vs Zero-Shot

AspectZero-ShotFew-Shot
Output formatFree-form paragraphStructured, mirrors examples
DepthHigh — adds contextMedium — stays in format
Hallucination riskHigherLower
ReproducibilityVaries across runsMore consistent

Technique 3 — Chain-of-Thought Prompting

We asked the LLM to reason step by step before giving its final answer. This is the most structured approach and produces the most traceable output.

The Prompt

Think step by step. Given these triples: [YOUR TRIPLES]

Step 1: List every entity assigned the Victim role. Note which language each comes from.
Step 2: List every entity assigned the Assailant role. Note which language.
Step 3: Are there roles that appear only in one language and not the others?
Step 4: Based on steps 1–3, what is your conclusion about how narrative framing of Jewish refugees differs across these sources?

Why Chain-of-Thought Works Best Here

Best for this topic
Chain-of-thought forces the LLM to ground each claim in a specific triple before drawing a conclusion. This dramatically reduces hallucination and makes it easier for us to verify which claims are supported by the data and which are not.

KG-only vs LLM-only vs RAG

We also compared three settings: structured SPARQL output alone, LLM asked the same question with no triples, and LLM given the triples (RAG).

SettingWhat we didKey observation
KG-onlyRead SPARQL result tables directlyPrecise but no narrative — numbers only, hard to interpret
LLM-onlyAsked LLM "how were Jewish refugees framed in 1938 press?" with NO triplesFluent, historically contextualised, but based on training data — not our corpus
RAGInjected 15 triples into LLM promptMore accurate, grounded in actual corpus data, better for academic use
Our main finding
LLM-only answers about Jewish persecution in 1938 are heavily influenced by the LLM's training data (e.g. general Holocaust knowledge) and not by our specific corpus. RAG significantly improves grounding — the LLM answers about these specific articles rather than about history in general.
